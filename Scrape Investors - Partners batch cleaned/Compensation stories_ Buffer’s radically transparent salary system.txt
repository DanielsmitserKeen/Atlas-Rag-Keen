## Buffer‚Äôs Compensation Philosophy

Buffer‚Äôs [compensation philosophy](https://ravio.com/blog/compensation-philosophy-guide) is made up of 4 key principles that guide how compensation decisions are made:¬†

1.  **Transparent** ‚Äì the compensation approach and individual salaries are shared to ensure accountability, build trust, and serve as a valuable resource for the wider industry.
2.  **Simple** ‚Äì ensuring it‚Äôs easy to understand how an individual‚Äôs salary is defined.
3.  **Fair** ‚Äì team members with the same role, responsibilities, and experience level are paid consistently and equitably.¬†
4.  **Generous** ‚Äì employees are compensated at above the market average to attract the right talent and ensure all team members have the means to thrive.

![Buffer's compensation philosophy](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6uJ7c43Q3f-ZA_Buffercompensationphilosophy.png?auto=format,compress)

These compensation principles are derived from [Buffer‚Äôs overall company values](https://buffer.com/about#values]), which are:

1.  Default to transparency
2.  Improve consistently
3.  Be a no-ego doer
4.  Show gratitude
5.  Choose optimism
6.  Reflect to unblock.

Buffer‚Äôs company values tell a story of striving to create a workplace that employees genuinely want to be part of, one where open and honest communication, optimism, reflective time, diverse perspectives, high-level thinking, and humility are embedded in day-to-day work ‚Äì all of which culminates in the gradual improvement of the team and, therefore, the product.

Transparency is central to Buffer‚Äôs compensation philosophy ‚Äì and they haven‚Äôt held back on putting the principle into practice.

So why does this fully open approach make sense for Buffer?

### Why does radical salary transparency make sense for Buffer?

A [compensation strategy](https://ravio.com/blog/compensation-strategy-advice) should always be tailored to fit the unique needs and culture of an individual company.¬†

So, whilst open salaries are right for Buffer, that doesn‚Äôt mean it‚Äôs the best choice for every business.

The key reasons that an open salary system makes sense for Buffer are:

*   **Open company, open salaries.** As we‚Äôve seen, transparency is a core tenet of Buffer, with the company making many metrics and processes externally visible that would traditionally be kept private. This stems from the company‚Äôs belief that transparency builds trust, holds the leadership team accountable, and drives the industry forward. An open salary system is a natural extension of this for Buffer.
*   **Pay equity as a company priority.** ‚ÄòFair‚Äô is another of Buffer‚Äôs compensation philosophy principles: employees who have the same role, responsibilities, and level of experience are paid equitably. Pay equity and pay transparency are closely intertwined because when compensation decisions are visible, employees gain more control and are able to identify any discrepancies and bias that may be influencing their pay (hence new laws like the [EU Pay Transparency Directive](https://ravio.com/blog/everything-you-need-to-know-about-the-eu-pay-transparency-directive)).
*   **Company size.** It‚Äôs also important to note that Buffer is a relatively small company: as of January 2025 Buffer has 75 employees in 30 different roles. Establishing and maintaining a transparent process and public salary database is always going to be much more manageable in a small and agile company.¬†

### The downsides of Buffer‚Äôs transparent salaries

When [announcing Buffer‚Äôs updated salary system](https://buffer.com/resources/salary-system/) _(explored in detail in the next section of this article)_, Buffer‚Äôs CEO Joel Gascoigne outlined a few disadvantages of the company‚Äôs salary transparency commitment:

*   **Additional work.** The need to design and maintain a compensation approach that the leadership team is accountable to and happy to have scrutinised by existing employees, job candidates, and the general public means close involvement in the process and details.
*   **Slow decisions and lack of flexibility.** If an issue with compensation arises or a new element is added (e.g. a brand new role added to the taxonomy) then the whole system must be checked and improved, which can slow down processes and decision-making ‚Äì as well as adding further work, as above.
*   **Additional costs.** Committing to fair and generous salaries for all employees inevitably means higher payroll costs ‚Äì and having this information public holds the leadership accountable to maintaining that commitment regardless of budget.
*   **Employee hesitancy on public salaries.** Some employees or new hires have been reluctant to have their personal salary shared openly, which can be difficult to handle.

These downsides of open salaries are a useful reminder that whilst the system works for Buffer, it isn‚Äôt right for every company.

## How salaries are calculated at Buffer

Buffer‚Äôs open salary database shows individual employee salaries ‚Äì but how are those salaries actually determined?

Luckily, Buffer also has publicly available information on how their salary system works, so we can explore that question in-depth.

For a long time, Buffer used a [simple salary formula](https://buffer.com/resources/compensation-philosophy/) to determine individual salaries, using [Radford‚Äôs](https://ravio.com/blog/radford-alternatives-for-compensation-management) median salary benchmark for San Francisco for each role, and multiplying this by a cost of living differential to account for employee location.¬†

Over time, this formula became too simplistic to account for all compensation needs, and inconsistencies started to creep in between teams.¬†

So in 2024 Buffer [announced a new ‚Äòsalary system‚Äô](https://buffer.com/resources/salary-system) with the aim of ensuring consistent and fair compensation for all employees.

Buffer salaries are now calculated using the following formula:

![Buffer's salary formula: (Buffer benchmark + COL multiplier) + salary choice](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6wZ7c43Q3f-ZJ_Buffersalarysystemnotion-1.png?auto=format,compress)

The ‚ÄòBuffer benchmark‚Äô calculation is key to how this works ‚Äì let‚Äôs take a closer look.

### Step 1: Market benchmarks form the foundation

Buffer uses [salary benchmarking data](https://ravio.com/blog/what-is-salary-benchmarking) by [Carta](https://ravio.com/blog/the-best-tools-for-salary-benchmarking) to gauge the current compensation market, aiming to ensure that fair and competitive compensation lies at the foundation of the calculation.¬†

Buffer filters the benchmarking data as follows:

*   The benchmarking data is filtered for company size, using Carta‚Äôs data pool for $10-25M company revenue.
*   The market benchmark for each role in Buffer‚Äôs taxonomy at level 5 is used as the foundation ‚Äì this is mid-career, as Carta‚Äôs level framework runs from 1-11 (and Buffer has adopted the same levels internally)¬†
*   Buffer looks at the benchmarks within the 70th-90th percentile for each role to [lead the market on pay](https://ravio.com/blog/lead-the-market-pay-strategy) ‚Äì the target percentile used for the benchmark currently varies per role as Buffer previously used [Radford‚Äôs benchmarking data](https://ravio.com/blog/radford-alternatives-for-compensation-management) at the 50th percentile, so this variance is to ensure consistency in employee salaries with this switch of data provider, with the aim over time to bring all employees to a consistent percentile.

![](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6xZ7c43Q3f-ZO_Buffer%E2%80%99stargetpercentilesperfunction.png?auto=format,compress)

### **üïµÔ∏è Ravio POV: Is Carta a reliable salary benchmarking data source?**

In their [documentation on how market benchmarks are used](https://buffer.notion.site/Buffer-Benchmarks-c664b9faea39427eaa54c8c489243f7d), Buffer highlights that they have found data gaps within the data they use from Carta ‚Äì which is the primary reason that they create their own ‚ÄòBuffer benchmarks‚Äô.

_‚ÄúWe start to run into data issues due to low sample size at certain levels. This inconsistency of sample size leads to significant variance in salaries over time as we pull fresh market data‚Ä¶The Buffer Benchmark helps address the challenges of sample size.‚Äù_

They use Carta‚Äôs level 5 as the foundational market data because _‚Äúthe data appeared to be most consistent at mid-levels‚Äù._

Carta‚Äôs core software is for managing [equity compensation](https://ravio.com/blog/the-complete-guide-to-equity-compensation-for-startups) for privately held, VC-backed startups, mostly US-based. From this, they harnessed their existing database of equity data as the starting point to build their compensation benchmarking database.

However, their core focus on privately held startups with a small headcount means that the data typically skews low due to the small headcount and early stage of these companies ‚Äì the database has [500,000 datapoints](https://carta.com/data/startup-compensation-h1-2024/#methodology) (i.e. employee salaries) from 45,000 companies, making the average headcount just 11 employees.

Buffer is a privately held startup with a relatively small team of 75 employees, so this dataset does align with the company‚Äôs size and stage ‚Äì an important consideration when evaluating [compensation data providers.](https://ravio.com/blog/the-best-tools-for-salary-benchmarking)¬†

### Step 2: Extrapolate pay at all levels based on standard employee progression at Buffer

Buffer‚Äôs career growth model is then applied to the level 5 market benchmark to determine the difference in each salary for the role.

There are two standardised paths for career progression at Buffer: standard growth, and fast early growth. Fast early growth is used for all roles in Finance or Advocacy (their customer support function) in recognition of the fact that there is a lower entry level for these functions and, therefore, fast progression through the first few job levels.

![Buffer's career progression framework](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6xJ7c43Q3f-ZN_Buffer%E2%80%99stargetpercentilesperfunction-1.png?auto=format,compress)

Here's a hypothetical example to put this into context.¬†

Let‚Äôs say Buffer is calculating salaries for each level of their Software Engineering role and the market benchmark at level 5 at the chosen percentile is $150,000.

Buffer would then determine the salary at each other level by applying the career growth model:

*   Level 1: $87,432 (inverse percentage calculation)
*   Level 2: $104,918 (inverse percentage calculation)
*   Level 3: $120,656 (inverse percentage calculation)
*   Level 4: $135,135 (inverse percentage calculation)
*   **Level 5: $150,000 (market benchmark)**
*   Level 6: $168,000 ($150,000 + 12%)
*   Level 7: $191,520 ($168,000 + 14%)
*   Level 8: $214,502 ($191,520 + 12%)
*   Level 9: $227,372 ($214,502 + 6%)
*   Level 10: $241,014 ($227,372 + 6%)
*   Level 11: $255,475 ($241,014 + 6%)

The resulting salaries are the ‚ÄòBuffer benchmarks‚Äô.

Whilst a standardised career progression model like this seems logical, the set [salary increases](https://ravio.com/blog/average-pay-rise) at promotion won‚Äôt always reflect the reality of fair and competitive pay.¬†

The reality is that the market moves independently for [each job level](https://ravio.com/blog/how-to-create-a-best-practice-job-levelling-framework) ‚Äì so the salary for each level should always be determined using [up-to-date market benchmarks](https://ravio.com/compensation-benchmarking) for that level.

Without this market reference point, over time employee compensation for some team members will fall behind market rates. This could cause heightened attrition, as employees look elsewhere to increase their salary, so it‚Äôs something to be careful about in your compensation approach.¬†

In Buffer‚Äôs case, this shouldn‚Äôt be too much of an issue.

Their compensation philosophy is deliberately ‚Äògenerous‚Äô so the benchmarks they do use are focused on paying above market rates.¬†

Plus, as we‚Äôll see later in this article, Buffer also regularly refreshes the underlying benchmarking data for each role, so if there are significant market shifts happening, they should be alerted to this before it becomes an issue.

However, for most companies, we wouldn‚Äôt recommend this approach of using a market benchmark for one level to extrapolate all other levels.

### Step 3: Apply cost of living multiplier¬†

A cost of living multiplier is then applied to the salary to account for an individual employee‚Äôs location.

Buffer uses San Francisco as the core location for making [cost of living comparisons](https://buffer.com/resources/location-independent-salaries/). They compare the cost of living in each employee‚Äôs location to San Francisco (a city known to have a very high cost of living), using [Numbeo‚Äôs Cost of Living Index](https://www.numbeo.com/cost-of-living/rankings.jsp).¬†

Numbeo‚Äôs COL Index uses New York as the base city, represented as 100 in the index. San Francisco is rated at 90.5, meaning that cost of living is 9.5% lower in San Francisco than in New York.

Included in Buffer‚Äôs ‚Äòhigh cost of living‚Äô band are: Zurich (104), New York (100), San Francisco (90.5), Seattle (81), Washington (81), London (78), Los Angeles (77).¬†

Buffer doesn't explain exactly how they decide which locations have a high cost of living compared to San Francisco, and which aren‚Äôt ‚Äì but we can infer from the above list that the cut off point is somewhere around Los Angeles at 77% of New York‚Äôs cost of living.

Employees in these locations receive a salary at 100%.

All other locations have a 90% cost of living multiplier applied to account for the lower cost of living compared to colleagues ‚Äì aiming to keep pay equitable across the team.¬†

So, to continue with the same example, a Buffer level 5 software engineer based in San Francisco would have a base salary of $150,000, but one based in Germany would have a base salary of $135,000 (90% of $150,000).

### **üïµÔ∏è Ravio POV: The problem with cost of living multipliers**

Many companies choose a [location-based pay model](https://ravio.com/blog/3-approaches-to-location-based-pay), wherein employee salaries differ depending on their location ‚Äì relevant both to large global companies who have teams based in several office locations, and also to [remote or hybrid companies](https://ravio.com/blog/employee-benefits-trends) who have employees working remotely in locations outside of the company HQ.¬†

Competitive market pay varies depending on location, so reflecting this within employee compensation is a strong positive.

However, many companies determine the salary for one location and then rely on cost of living adjustments to calculate how salaries should differ across locations.¬†

This is exactly what Buffer is doing ‚Äì using benchmarking data for San Francisco, USA, to base all other locations upon, with a 90% cost of living multiplier for locations with a lower cost of living.

Localising pay is hard and there are a large number of nuanced factors that go into global salary variances. Cost of living is just one factor, alongside others like supply vs demand for a specific role and location ‚Äì which can actually have much more of an impact on market data. Therefore, relying on cost of living alone to determine salary differences per location is unreliable.¬†

Furthermore, it‚Äôs also very hard to objectively measure the cost of living in different locations. This is especially true outside of major cities, where data is not readily available in databases like Numbeo‚Äôs.

Plus, using manual cost of living calculations and building your own benchmarks in spreadsheets (like Buffer is) is time-consuming, unscalable, and leaves a lot of room for error.

Instead, the best practice approach here would be to use compensation benchmarking data for each location, by a provider like [Ravio](http://ravio.com/benchmarking).

Ravio transforms the way you approach compensation, making it easy to build compelling packages in any market. With live, accurate compensation insights, you can align decisions with global market standards across 45+ countries, and understand local benchmarks relative to your HQ base location ‚Äì all without the hassle of spreadsheets or unreliable cost of living calculations.

### Step 4: Apply ‚Äòsalary choice‚Äô

The final part of Buffer‚Äôs salary calculation is ‚ÄòSalary Choice‚Äô.¬†

All new employees at Buffer previously had the option to optimise their compensation package for cash or [equity](https://ravio.com/blog/the-complete-guide-to-equity-compensation-for-startups) ‚Äì either they‚Äôd be granted additional stock options, or have an additional $10,000 added to their base salary.

This is no longer an option in the new evolution of Buffer‚Äôs salary system and is being phased out, but it does still apply to several existing employees and so remains part of the salary calculation for now.

### What is the CEO of Buffer‚Äôs salary?

The base salary of Joel Gascoigne, CEO of Buffer, is currently **$298,958** per year.

![CEO salary at Buffer](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6xp7c43Q3f-ZP_CEOsalaryatBuffer.png?auto=format,compress)

The [CEO salary at Buffer](https://buffer.notion.site/CEO-Salary-3d88f5808df94f32b1002bab773cdbaf) is a deliberate departure from the overall salary system.

Buffer primarily ties CEO pay to company performance, aiming to limit factors and discrepancies that would mean that company executive pay vastly outpaces that of the rest of the team.¬†

To enforce this, the CEO salary is a more simple calculation: CEO benchmark x cost of living multiplier ‚Äì and Joel Gascoigne is based in Boulder, so has the 90% cost of living multiplier applied to his salary.¬†

There are no job levels involved and the percentile used for the benchmark is consistent with all other team members (currently between 70th-90th, in future all will be the same percentile).¬†

This effectively means that the only way that CEO pay can increase is:

*   Company revenue increases and so the company size filter applied when benchmarking is changed ‚Äì directly connected to company performance, and the change would also see a salary increase for all employees
*   The market benchmark used for the CEO increases
*   The target percentile used for benchmarking is changed ‚Äì again, this would be changed across all employees.

However, it‚Äôs also important to note that as the founder of Buffer, [Joel Gascoigne also has a significant equity stake](https://buffer.notion.site/Ownership-ea68c64db76a4914b4103738fbf1faf5) in the company, which is where his main economic upside comes from.

Joel currently holds 56.78% of issued shares in Buffer ‚Äì a significant majority. Investors hold 38.25%, ex-employees 3.87%, and current employees 1.09%.

![Buffer equity pool ‚Äì founder vs investors vs employees](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6uZ7c43Q3f-ZB_Bufferequity.png?auto=format,compress)

There‚Äôs no information given on whether the CEO / founder is eligible for [equity refresh grants](https://ravio.com/blog/how-to-use-equity-refresh-grants-for-employee-loyalty-and-retention) or other elements of compensation which may bolster the base salary.

### Can new hires at Buffer negotiate their salary?

Salary negotiation is not allowed during the Buffer hiring process.¬†

This is in order to maintain consistency in how individual employee salaries are calculated ‚Äì for Buffer, salary negotiation is a key time when discrepancies arise that benefit individual team members, leading to pay equity issues, and so this has been eliminated.

### **üïµÔ∏è Ravio POV: Does salary negotiation impact pay equity?**

The gender pay gap is most likely to arise during the hiring process ‚Äì new hire salaries for women are systematically lower than for men performing work of the same value.

This was a key finding of our [Ravio Pay Equity Report](https://ravio.com/pay-equity-report-2024).

To give one example, we found that for P4 Software Engineers in the UK the median new hire salary for men is ¬£85,000 whereas for women it is ¬£82,288 ‚Äì an adjusted pay gap of 3.3%.

In contrast, there is no statistically significant difference in promotion rates or promotion pay increases between men and women.

This indicates that discriminatory hiring practices are leading to lower new hire salaries for women ‚Äì and the [salary negotiation process is absolutely part of that](https://www.pon.harvard.edu/daily/salary-negotiations/the-backlash-effect-for-women-negotiators-in-hollywood-and-beyond-nb/). So, Buffer‚Äôs no salary negotiation policy definitely makes sense for eliminating discrepancies.¬†

## How compensation reviews work at Buffer

At Buffer, [compensation reviews](https://ravio.com/blog/complete-guide-to-compensation-reviews) focus on [‚Äòrebenchmarking‚Äô](https://buffer.notion.site/Rebenchmarking-1c0df96cdb4240a68d711266236931de) i.e. updating the salary benchmarking data that is used within salary calculations, to reflect any changes in the market and ensure employee salaries keep up with market rates.

Buffer‚Äôs benchmarking data is currently updated once a quarter, and the Buffer benchmarks are updated twice a year (June and December) to reflect any market changes, with individual salaries adjusted at this point if required.

There is no [pay for performance](https://ravio.com/blog/pay-for-performance-the-pros-and-cons) element in Buffer‚Äôs compensation review process. Again, this is to maintain consistency ‚Äì salaries are only increased due to [market adjustments](https://ravio.com/blog/market-adjustment-raises), where changes are made to the underlying salary calculations, or due to career progression for individual employees.

### **üïµÔ∏è Ravio POV: Does the choice not to reward performance put employee motivation and retention at risk?**

The benefits of a [pay for performance model](https://ravio.com/blog/pay-for-performance-the-pros-and-cons) are:

*   Supporting the retention of top talent by recognising and rewarding them for their contributions
*   Increasing employee motivation and improves output through incentivising high performance
*   Improving strategic alignment through connecting business goals and priorities with employee rewards.

So, Buffer does potentially put motivation and retention at risk by not including performance within their compensation review cycle.

However, Buffer already has a ‚Äògenerous‚Äô compensation philosophy, paying above the market and regularly adjusting salaries based on market changes. Plus, Buffer employees also receive equity compensation (_see section below)_, which naturally incentivises them to perform well to ensure the business is successful ‚Äì increasing the upside potential of their equity.

Therefore, that motivation and retention risk is less likely here, and as we saw earlier in the article, it does seem that Buffer has a much higher than average employee tenure.

Pay for performance does have disadvantages too.

[Studies](https://ideas.repec.org/a/iza/izawol/journl2022n152.html) have found that it can encourage a competitive, individualistic, high-stress culture as employees are incentivised to meet targets before pay reviews.

It can also cause pay equity issues and inconsistencies if [approached incorrectly](https://ravio.com/blog/how-to-ensure-merit-increases-are-effective) ‚Äì which, given their core principles, is likely why Buffer avoids rewarding performance. Rewarding performance inevitably relies on subjective performance reviews to an extent, which can introduce bias and, therefore, pay inconsistencies and inequities.

## How career progression works at Buffer

Buffer‚Äôs new salary system (from 2024 onwards) also included the introduction of a new level framework.¬†

Previously, each department had autonomy over how job levels and titles were used, but this was leading to inconsistency and confusion, so standardised levels are now in place.

Here‚Äôs a snapshot of the framework, and the full descriptions can be found in [Buffer‚Äôs salary system Notion wiki.](https://buffer.notion.site/Buffer-Career-Framework-Levels-Steps-2c37beef51ff407599c36a463cec3113)

![Buffer's level framework](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6w57c43Q3f-ZM_Buffer%E2%80%99slevelframework.png?auto=format,compress)

Each level also has two ‚Äòsteps‚Äô within it, to enable career progression within an employee‚Äôs existing level to be recognised:

*   **Step 1:** The default step for all employees who are fulfilling expectations for their level.¬†
*   **Step 2:** The ‚Äòhalfway point‚Äô between one level and the next, to recognise employees who are starting to show the skills and impact expected at the next level and are very experienced within their level.

No details are given on the expected timeline in terms of how long it takes a typical Buffer employee to move from step 1 to step 2, to a promotion to the next level.¬†

In terms of pay progression at Buffer, then, there are three ways that employee salaries can increase: market adjustments during rebenchmarking, underlying changes made to the salary system e.g. [target percentile](https://ravio.com/blog/the-compensation-metrics-every-hr-team-should-be-tracking) increased, company size filter increased, salary calculation changed, or a promotion to the next step or level.

## Total Rewards at Buffer

Buffer is well known for their transparent salaries, so much of this example has focused on how salaries work at Buffer.

But salary is just one element of compensation.¬†

So, what does total compensation look like at Buffer?

### Employee equity at Buffer

Buffer does offer equity compensation to all employees on top of base salary, but the [stock option approach](https://buffer.notion.site/Our-approach-to-stock-options-923abb3ff51a4b4689f731f4e6f46488) is currently being re-designed and there has been no public announcement about this yet.

The information on the Buffer Notion wiki suggests that there will be three elements to equity compensation at Buffer:

1.  A new hire grant for all new joiners
2.  Promotion grant issued at the point of promotion to reflect increased impact¬†
3.  Evergreen grant issued at pre-defined employee tenure milestones to encourage employee retention and loyalty.¬†

### Profit-sharing bonuses at Buffer

Since 2017 Buffer has had a profit sharing programme in place, where employees are rewarded [profit share bonuses](https://ravio.com/blog/employee-bonuses-pros-and-cons) to share in the financial success of the company.¬†

Employees received these profit share bonuses from 2017-2021, but did not in 2022 and 2023 because the company was not profitable in those years.¬†

In 2024 Buffer [returned to profitability](https://buffer.com/resources/2024-profit-share/), and so employees received their profit share bonus.¬†

![Buffer profit share bonuses ‚Äì employee reaction](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6vp7c43Q3f-ZG_bufferprofitsharebonuses.png?auto=format,compress)

15% of the company‚Äôs net profit was distributed to employees through this bonus structure, totalling around $30,000 for 2024.

With 73 team members, this gave an average bonus of $416 per employee ‚Äì but the exact amount did vary depending on the following factors:¬†

*   40% was distributed equally across all team members
*   40% was distributed weighted by base salary, to reflect the level of responsibility and impact of each team member
*   20% was distributed weighted by tenure, to reflect the value of long-term commitment.

![Buffer profit share numbers for 2024](https://images.prismic.io/ravio/Z7X6v57c43Q3f-ZI_bufferprofitsharenumbers.png?auto=format,compress)

### Employee benefits and perks at Buffer

Flexibility and work/life balance is a clear priority for [Buffer‚Äôs employee benefits package](https://buffer.com/journey), including:

*   **100% remote:** employees are free to live and work wherever they like
*   **4 day work week:** Buffer adopted a 4 day work week as an experiment in 2020 and has maintained it due to the [hugely positive impacts](https://buffer.com/resources/4dww/) on employee health and wellbeing.
*   **Office holiday closure**: all Buffer employees [take the last week of the year off](https://buffer.com/resources/closed-for-the-holidays/) as an opportunity to recharge and reset without relying on holiday allowance¬†
*   **Sabbaticals**: once employees reach their 5 year Buffer anniversary they are able to take a fully paid 6 week sabbatical¬†
*   **Home office stipend**: a one-off $1,000 stipend to spend on home office improvements
*   **Working smarter stipend**: additional funds for home working needs e.g. paying for a co-working space.

Buffer also has particularly strong [benefits](https://ravio.com/blog/employee-benefits-guide) for parents, which, given [the role of childbearing on the gender pay gap](https://ravio.com/blog/3-reasons-why-there-is-a-gender-pay-gap), aligns with the company‚Äôs focus on fair and equitable pay:

*   **Dependent stipend**: all employees with dependent children are granted an additional $250 per month per child in recognition of the significant additional expense of raising a family ‚Äì this is issued separately from salary to maintain consistency
*   **Family leave**: all parents receive 16 weeks of fully paid family leave.

## The future of compensation at Buffer

Buffer plans to continue to refine and improve the salary system over time, to continue to simplify the approach and further align with the guiding principles of the company‚Äôs compensation philosophy.

As it stands, [the top priorities for future adjustments](https://buffer.notion.site/Future-Iterations-896bdd5e299f482ea661cafe23e0343a) include:

*   **Location-independent salaries.** Removing the cost of living multiplier from salary calculations and instead pay all Buffer employees at the high cost of living rate.
*   **Consistent target percentile.** Align the target percentile used across all departments to bring consistency. Two scenarios are being explored: bringing all employees to the 80th percentile (additional payroll cost of $314,467) or to the 85th percentile ($802,551) ‚Äì something that would be easy to investigate using the scenario modelling feature in [Ravio‚Äôs salary bands software](https://ravio.com/bands).
*   **Eliminate ‚Äòsalary choice‚Äô**. Whereas many companies are exploring more flexible compensation models, Buffer has removed the ability for new hires to opt for additional stock options or additional base salary. In time, Buffer will also remove the additional base salary from employees who chose this option at the time, to bring further consistency ‚Äì likely at the same time as they move to location-independent salaries.¬†
