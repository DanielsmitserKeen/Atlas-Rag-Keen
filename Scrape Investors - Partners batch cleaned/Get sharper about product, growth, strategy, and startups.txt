A lot has been said about us losing our jobs to AI.

The argument for why we would is pretty simple: We‚Äôre literally inventing AI to do stuff for us, and it‚Äôs getting better everyday. And by continuously automating our labour, that puts us on some trajectory to eventually have nothing to do, right? Simply‚Ä¶

[

![](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Bacr!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa785391f-ab3f-4d26-b9d0-1abb98743184_1512x657.jpeg)

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!Bacr!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2Fa785391f-ab3f-4d26-b9d0-1abb98743184_1512x657.jpeg)

The same argument of human labour being squeeze by AI implies, because we live in a supply-and-demand world, that the surplus of humans looking for something to do will bring down wages. So‚Ä¶.AI automation leads to less jobs, and less pay once you find one.

Sure, we could invent new things for us to do (√† la the Agricultural and Industrial Revolutions), but that will take a lot more time to figure out (and retrain ourselves) than it will for AI to be capable of replacing you.

Gloomy news for a Friday morning‚Ä¶.

_Well_, thank the Pope, not so fast. [Noah Smith](https://open.substack.com/users/8243895-noah-smith?utm_source=mentions) raises an excellent point **[in this essay](https://www.noahpinion.blog/p/plentiful-high-paying-jobs-in-the)** [](https://www.noahpinion.blog/p/plentiful-high-paying-jobs-in-the)that makes me feel a lot happier: **While AI certainly will be able to do most conceivable things better than you, it won‚Äôt make sense for it to because of a two simple yet powerful principles, (1)** _**comparative**_ **advantage, and (2) opportunity cost.**

The result, **we** _**should**_ **have plentiful, high-paying jobs in the age of AI dominance** ‚Äî probably doing **much the same kind of work that we‚Äôre doing right now.**

> When most people hear the term ‚Äúcomparative advantage‚Äù for the first time, they immediately think of the wrong thing. They think the term means something along the lines of ‚Äúwho can do a thing better‚Äù. After all, if an AI is better than you at storytelling, or reading an MRI, it‚Äôs better _compared_ to you, right? Except that‚Äôs not actually what comparative advantage means. The term for ‚Äúwho can do a thing better‚Äù is ‚Äú_competitive_ advantage‚Äù, or ‚Äúabsolute advantage‚Äù.
> 
> **Comparative advantage** actually means ‚Äúwho can do a thing better relative to the other things they can do‚Äù. So for example, suppose I‚Äôm worse than everyone at everything, but I‚Äôm a little _less_ bad at drawing portraits than I am at anything else. I don‚Äôt have any _competitive_ advantages at all, but drawing portraits is my _comparative_ advantage.
> 
> The key difference here is that everyone ‚Äî every single person, every single AI, _everyone_ ‚Äî always has a comparative advantage at _something!_To help illustrate this fact, let‚Äôs look at a simple example \[‚Ä¶\] illustrating how someone can get paid ‚Äî and paid _well_ ‚Äî to do a job that the person hiring them would actually be _better at doing_:
> 
> > _Imagine a venture capitalist (let‚Äôs call him ‚ÄúMarc‚Äù) who is an almost inhumanly fast typist. He‚Äôll still hire a secretary to draft letters for him, though, because even if that secretary is a slower typist than him, Marc can generate more value using his time to do something other than drafting letters. So he ends up paying someone else to do something that he‚Äôs actually better at._
> 
>   
> _‚Äî Noah Smith, via_ [Noahpinion](https://open.substack.com/pub/noahpinion)

**Comparative advantage is already everywhere**. At work (or at home where you divvy up responsibilities with your partner), everyone ends up doing the thing they‚Äôre best at _relative to the other things they could be doing_, rather than the thing they‚Äôre best at _relative to other people_.

This all boils down as Noah points out to **producer-specific constraints**, which is significant in the context of AI and our jobs, because the _computing power_ necessary for AI to work is limited and expensive.

Yes, compute is on a path to getting more plentiful and cheaper, but it will always be finite, meaning there is a limit on amount of AI-executed tasks.

Which leads to **the opportunity cost of AI‚Äôs time**. As Noah explains, ‚ÄúWhen AI becomes so powerful that it can be used for practically anything, the cost of using AI for any task will be determined by _the value of the other things the AI could be used for instead_.‚Äù

Now, outside of just learning a new perspective, what can you practically do with this concept? üõ†Ô∏è

Practice switching your thinking in terms of _competitive_ advantage to thinking in terms of _comparative_ advantage. This can bring you a more nuanced approach to strategy, market positioning, and how you use your team resources. Tactically, this means:

*   If you‚Äôre a manager, get the maximum leverage from your team by considering how to delegate based on relative strengths and weakness.
    
*   Think about not just your product‚Äôs strengths, but how those strengths compare to its other capabilities and the market at large, allowing for more precise market positioning.

I have mixed feelings at the moment on raising VC money. I always thought it was given step when building a company, but my views have continued to change and lean towards building bootstrapped.

But that‚Äôs absolutely a personal thought and just relates to how I‚Äôd want to build a businesses if I ever did it again.

There are of course many reasons to raise money, and in a recent essay, [Mario Gabriele](https://open.substack.com/users/9653721-mario-gabriele?utm_source=mentions) spoke to 8 founders who‚Äôve collectively raised over $4B to share some concrete lessons. I really enjoyed the advice‚Ä¶

> Fundraising is a skill. At the earliest stages, storytelling ability is a big differentiator between being a successful founder and an unsuccessful one. When your startup is at an early stage, you often don‚Äôt have metrics or even a product to point to, so you have to be able to tell a narrative about what you‚Äôre doing and why you‚Äôre doing it. For many founders, storytelling doesn‚Äôt come easily, but it‚Äôs a skill you can build. In later stages, company metrics drive most investment decisions. There are a lot of really smart growth investors out there, and if you feel that you‚Äôre getting shut down by them, ask them to be specific about the elements of your company‚Äôs metrics that are causing them to be cynical about an investment.¬†
> 
> Founders often treat fundraising like a sales process where they try to use leverage and create scarcity/urgency. In my experience, this doesn‚Äôt work. Scarcity and demand are important to driving an oversubscribed process to completion, but those rounds don‚Äôt become high demand because the founder has created the _illusion_ of high demand. They become high demand because they are companies that people are excited to invest in. It‚Äôs obvious to investors when a founder is playing a game (though it‚Äôs often not obvious to the founder how transparent they‚Äôre being). It‚Äôs incredibly off-putting to investors when they realize you‚Äôve inserted artificial scarcity into a process.¬†
> 
> _‚Äî Trae Stephens (Exec Chairman & Co-founder at Anduril), via_ [The Generalist](https://open.substack.com/pub/thegeneralist)

*   Keep investors warm with build-in-public updates and casual talks
    
*   Run a tight process to raise as fast as you can
    
*   Don‚Äôt bullshit and create artificial urgency, it will be sniffed out
    
*   Raise on a great story, or strong metrics ‚Äì not both
    
*   Have a strong narrative of your ‚Äúwhy now‚Äù and your unique advantage
    
*   The best way to get investment is to not need investment
    
*   Optimize for the right partners, not the highest valuation
    
*   Know your business‚Äôs risks, ordered from high to low
    
*   Don‚Äôt get distracted by fundraising. Focus mostly on delivering customer value
    
*   You only need a few yeses

Crossing thresholds leads to exponential growth, which as Paul Graham (founder of Y Combinator) argues, leads to superlinear returns, which leads to a "winner take all‚Äù scenario.

[

![How to make a breakthrough ‚Äì Dimitri Glazkov](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!V8We!,w_1456,c_limit,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F79986225-aa9a-43ee-a51d-db59b71da168_616x428.png "How to make a breakthrough ‚Äì Dimitri Glazkov")

](https://substackcdn.com/image/fetch/$s_!V8We!,f_auto,q_auto:good,fl_progressive:steep/https%3A%2F%2Fsubstack-post-media.s3.amazonaws.com%2Fpublic%2Fimages%2F79986225-aa9a-43ee-a51d-db59b71da168_616x428.png)

ignore the math

> One of the most important things I didn't understand about the world when I was a child is the degree to which the returns for performance are superlinear.
> 
> Teachers and coaches implicitly told us the returns were linear. "You get out," I heard a thousand times, "what you put in." They meant well, but this is rarely true. If your product is only half as good as your competitor's, you don't get half as many customers. You get no customers, and you go out of business.
> 
> It's obviously true that the returns for performance are superlinear in business. Some think this is a flaw of capitalism, and that if we changed the rules it would stop being true. But superlinear returns for performance are a feature of the world, not an artifact of rules we've invented. We see the same pattern in fame, power, military victories, knowledge, and even benefit to humanity. In all of these, the rich get richer.
> 
> You can't understand the world without understanding the concept of superlinear returns. And if you're ambitious you definitely should, because this will be the wave you surf on.
> 
> _‚Äî Paul Graham_

**The first takeaway here is this‚Äî**absolute numbers are a vanity, distraction, and are generally unactionable. _Rates_ are what matters, and more specifically, _growth rates._

So, focus almost entirely on that metric, especially in the earlier innings of building or doing something measurable. For one, it will help prevent discouragement when the absolute numbers are low early on. But more importantly, it will help you prioritize what areas to focus on.

**The second takeaway is more broad and philosophical**. When picking a startup idea or deciding where you spend your time, try look for things that don‚Äôt have thresholds, but rather, a practically infinite potential to compound. And from that list, it‚Äôs key to pick things that you care a lot about, because, ‚Äú_The most obvious way to take advantage of superlinear returns for performance is by doing exceptionally good work, and to do something exceptionally well, you have to be interested in it.‚Äù_

There are two obvious ways work can compound.

1.  _**Directly**_, in that doing well in one cycle causes you to do better in the next. For example, building infrastructure or growing an audience
    
2.  _**By teaching you**_, in that learning has an unreachable ceiling. Even if feel you're doing badly while you‚Äôre doing something, if you're building knowledge, then you're getting exponential growth.
    

That‚Äôs because knowledge grows exponentially. So, go forth and **always be learning**. If you're not, you're probably not on a path that leads to superlinear returns.

A lot has been written about how to do strategy well. And with it, a lot of frameworks and templates have been pumped out and pushed, all promising that if followed, will bring you a brilliant strategy.

I‚Äôve said it before, copying and pasting any template usually means key context specific to your product, team, and market, is missing.

However, **guiding questions are not a template. Rather, they help create a mental model for thinking strategically.** [Bandan Jot Singh](https://open.substack.com/users/18104096-bandan-jot-singh?utm_source=mentions) shared 10 questions that can help with just that.

> A strategy document serves as a comprehensive but sharp-like-knife narrative that effectively communicates the directions necessary to succeed in the targeted market.
> 
> It goes beyond merely informing leadership, but extending to all levels of the organization, such that team members at every level understand their role in contributing to the strategy.
> 
> _‚Äî Bandan, via_ [Productify by Bandan](https://open.substack.com/pub/productify)

Tactics are not strategy. Rather, strategy is a simple narrative that should be able to to be condensed to a memorable soundbite. Strategy is clear message that sets the directions necessary to succeed in the targeted market.

**To help craft that narrative, Bandan suggests answering this list of 10 questions as comprehensively and clearly as you can.**

1.  Where do we want to be in 3 years, and how does this goal compare to our current state as a company?
    
2.  Which market opportunities (and needs), do we need to address to achieve our goals, and why are these the right opportunities?
    
3.  Which target customers do we believe we can effectively serve within these opportunities? What are the problem areas of these people?
    
4.  What makes our organization better suited than others to address these opportunities?
    
5.  What specific actions will we take to maximize our chances of success in these opportunities?
    
6.  Which risks do we need to mitigate along the way, and how will we do so?
    
7.  Under what circumstances would we reconsider our chosen actions during the 3-year period?
    
8.  What distractions must we avoid to maintain focus on our selected actions?
    
9.  What support do we require to execute these actions effectively?
    
10.  How will we measure our progress and know that we are moving towards success?
