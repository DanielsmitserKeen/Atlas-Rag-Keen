**How healthy is your SaaS business?**

We‚Äôre bombarded with KPIs and an endless series of metrics to tell us how we‚Äôre doing.

But instead of using data to measure our progress, it‚Äôs much more likely that we get lost and start focusing on metrics that are easy to track but don‚Äôt mean anything.

For a SaaS business, there are a few core metrics that need your undivided attention. And the priority of these metrics shift as you grow. If you‚Äôve only had paying customers for 2 months, it doesn‚Äôt make much sense to track lifetime value. But later on, lifetime value is essential.

In this post, I‚Äôm going to break down the essential metrics for each stage of a SaaS business.

What this framework will give you:

*   By focusing on a few key metrics, you‚Äôll also be focusing on the core problems you need to solve to get your business to the next level.
    
*   Data doesn‚Äôt do you any good unless you act on it. Each of these metrics clearly tells you how you‚Äôre doing. Right away, you‚Äôll know where you need to spend your time.
    
*   Each stage has two metrics that balance each other. This keeps you from over-optimizing one metric and unintentionally harming the long-term health of your business.
    

Let‚Äôs jump in.

**Before Product/Market Fit  
**You‚Äôve just made the decision to start your business and you‚Äôve got plans for world domination.

But before you can start building your empire, you need to make sure you have the right product for the right market.

For most new products, there‚Äôs usually a disconnect at the beginning and customers don‚Äôt quite want what you have. Either you need to go after a different target market or you need to change your product to fit their needs. When you get this match, we call it product/market fit.

You‚Äôre probably in this stage if:

*   You‚Äôve just started.
    
*   You don‚Äôt know who your ideal customer is.
    
*   People are testing your product for the first time.
    

This is the first major hurdle you‚Äôll need to overcome. But how do we measure our progress when we don‚Äôt have any data? You don‚Äôt even have any paying customers at this point and if you do, it‚Äôs not many. At this point, running a bunch of A/B tests won‚Äôt help you test your business model.

Instead, you‚Äôll rely heavily on qualitative feedback and one critical survey question.

Primary Goals:

*   Validate core business assumptions by talking to people in your target market. If people ask you for your product before you even try to sell them, you‚Äôre going in the right direction.
    
*   Survey users and have at least 40% say that they‚Äòd be very disappointed if they had to stop using your product.
    

**Metric #1: Qualitative Feedback  
**Yes‚Ä¶ this isn‚Äôt technically a metric. But it‚Äôs too early for data anyway so you‚Äôll need to make the most of what you can get: feedback.

Right now, you really only have one goal: build the right product for the right market. And the fastest way to do this is to start talking to your customers.

If you have any users at this point, jump on Skype and get a deep understanding of their main problems. Ask them to show you how they currently solve the problem you‚Äôre going after. Then show them what you‚Äôve been working on to see if they get excited about it. Usually, you‚Äôll want to follow this format for the interview:

1.  Basic demographic questions to get a better sense for who you‚Äôre talking to.
    
2.  Deep questions about the current problem.
    
3.  Present your solution for feedback (don‚Äôt sell it, just get feedback).
    

You‚Äôll want to do 10-20 of these customer interviews.

If you don‚Äôt have any users at this point, go and talk to people that you think would want to use your product. This is a great way to start testing different target markets efficiently. It‚Äôs a lot easier to schedule 10 more Skype meetings than it is to rebuild or rebrand your product.

When you want to start scaling feedback (especially as you move into the later stages of your business), use [Qualaroo](https://qualaroo.com/) surveys, [SurveyMonkey](http://www.surveymonkey.com/), feedback forms, and usability tests like [UserTesting.com](http://www.usertesting.com/). But when you‚Äôre just starting, talk to people in your target market one-on-one. The insights will always be much better.

At KISSmetrics, we still do customer interviews each and every time we make a major change to our product. Adding a new feature? Go talk to customers. Revamping an old feature? Let‚Äôs talk to our customers that use it the most. Starting a new project like our [Google Analytics app](http://www.myanalyticsapp.com/)? Find a group of Google Analytics users to talk to. We do it every single time.

**Metric #2: Measuring Product/Market Fit  
**There‚Äôs just one little problem with all this customer feedback though.

It‚Äôs super difficult to measure objectively. Are people REALLY interested in our product or are we only focusing on the positive feedback while downplaying the negative feedback?

Luckily, there is a survey question that will help you quantify whether or not you‚Äôve reached product/market fit. [Full credit goes to Sean Ellis for this question](http://www.startup-marketing.com/using-survey-io/). Here it is:

How would you feel if you could no longer use \[product\]?

1.  Very disappointed
    
2.  Somewhat disappointed
    
3.  Not disappointed (it isn‚Äôt really that useful)
    
4.  N/A ‚Äì I no longer use \[product\]
    

Send this to people that have used your product at least twice, experienced your core product offering, and used it in the last two weeks. The goal is to get at least 40% of your users to say ‚Äúvery disappointed.‚Äù

If you don‚Äôt meet the 40% benchmark, you may need to reposition your product or pivot entirely. If you do hit it, time to move on to the next stage.

**Beginning to Scale  
**So you‚Äôve found product/market fit.

You‚Äôve got revenue coming in and a growing customer base. Now it‚Äôs time to build a business.

Up until this point, you didn‚Äôt really need to track much. Outside of basic user signups and revenue, there wasn‚Äôt anything to track. Now that you got the right product for the right market, there are two metrics that will keep you headed in the right direction.

You‚Äôre probably in this stage if:

*   You‚Äôve found at least one way to acquire customers consistently.
    
*   Many of your customers stay subscribed and want to keep paying you.
    
*   Your monthly revenue is starting to grow.
    

Primary Goals:

*   Consistently grow MRR while controlling churn.
    
*   Get monthly churn to 1-2%. If it‚Äôs above 5%, ignore everything else until you lower it.
    

**Metric #1: Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)  
**For a SaaS business, monthly recurring revenue is a much more valuable metric to track than traditional revenue. It‚Äôs the total revenue you received during the month that came from recurring subscriptions.

The health of a SaaS business heavily depends on recurring revenue. It can take months to regain the cost of acquiring a customer and the real profits come from increasing that subscription revenue. One-time windfalls just aren‚Äôt that valuable to us. By tracking monthly recurring revenue, we can see exactly how our business is doing month-to-month.

Unfortunately, tracking MRR can get tricky. There‚Äôs several use-cases that your tracking systems will need to be able to handle:

*   Having annual plans on top of your regular monthly plans complicates things a bit. The annual revenue actually needs to get divided between each month of the subscription, not just the month when the customer is billed.
    
*   Upgrades and downgrades get tedious to track. If a customer moves from a $10/month plan to a $50/month plan, you‚Äôll need to add an extra $40/month to your MRR.
    
*   You‚Äôll need to remove revenue when it churns with a cancellation.
    

Speaking of churn‚Ä¶

**Metric #2: Churn  
**Growing MRR is one side of the coin at this stage. The other side is churn. If you can‚Äôt keep customers subscribed, it won‚Äôt be long before your MRR won‚Äôt budge and your business will stall.

The thing is, churn can be a devious metric. At the beginning, a monthly churn rate of 10% doesn‚Äôt seem so bad. If you have 100 customers, 10 of them left. Not that big a deal right? It‚Äôs pretty easy to get 10 more customers. But what happens when you have 10,000 customers? Now 1,000 of them left in a single month. Even the best marketing machines have a hard time keeping up with something like that.

Your churn rate starts out innocent and easy to handle. But it can quickly get out of control if you‚Äôre not keeping a close eye on it. In order to build a strong foundation that will help your company grow over the long-term, you absolutely, without a doubt, NEED to get control of your churn rate.

So what‚Äôs a good churn?

It always varies by industry. But in general, it‚Äôs critical that you get your monthly churn under 5% and your goal should be 1-2%. Later on, you can experiment with upsells and cross-sells [to get negative churn](http://www.forentrepreneurs.com/why-churn-is-critical-in-saas/).

**Expansion  
**Sooner or later, you‚Äôre going to hit a wall.

The main channel you‚Äôve been using to acquire customers will start to slow down and you‚Äôll hit diminishing returns. If you want to keep growing each month, you‚Äôll need to find new sources of growth.

You might start testing affiliate programs, new ad networks, PR, business development, referral programs, new types of content marketing, conferences, event marketing, or whatever type of marketing happens to be hot at the moment. You‚Äôve got LOTS of options to choose from. Some of them will be a great fit for your market, others will fail completely.

You‚Äôre probably in this stage if:

*   Growth is beginning to slow for the first time.
    
*   Continuing to improve your main channel is getting a lot harder.
    
*   You‚Äôve successfully controlled your churn.
    

So as you start to experiment with new channels of growth, you need to focus heavily on two metrics. These metrics will keep your experiments in check and make sure you scale profitable channels.

Primary Goals:

*   Keep your cost per acquisition to one third of your lifetime value.
    
*   Get each customer to profitability within 12 months.
    

**Metric #1: Lifetime Value (LTV)  
**How much revenue do you earn in total from a customer before they leave your business? For a SaaS business, it‚Äôs absolutely critical to track lifetime value. When you factor in acquisition, support, and product costs, it can take a SaaS businesses 6-12 months to turn a profit on a customer.

To make sure customers stay long enough to keep your business healthy, we use lifetime value (some people abbreviate it as CLV or LCV).

By now, you‚Äôll have had customers long enough so that you can actually figure out your LTV. [Use the formula here](http://www.forentrepreneurs.com/saas-metrics-2-definitions/) to get started. When you have more resources, you might also want to include [second-order revenue](http://saastr.wordpress.com/2013/02/01/its-not-just-cltv-its-your-trgcltv-that-matters-total-all-in-revenue-generated-by-your-customer/) in your LTV calculation.

**Metric #2: Cost Per Acquisition  
**As we begin to experiment with new channels to keep growing, cost per acquisition keeps us in check. It‚Äôs the total cost it takes to acquire a customer from a particular source.

For the average CPA of your business, you can total up your entire marketing and sales expenses over a month then average that out over the total customers you acquired. But we need to take it a step further and segment CPA by acquisition channel. This tells us whether or not customers from new channels are worth the effort.

When you‚Äôre experimenting with new channels, it‚Äôll usually be pretty obvious if the math won‚Äôt work out. Bad channels tend to be BAD channels. So keep experimenting until you find the ones that work.

Not only will CPA help you evaluate new channels for growth, it‚Äôll help you figure out how far to push your main channels. How much can you actually spend to acquire a customer on AdWords or Facebook? How many writers can you hire to put together content? By keeping an eye on these metrics, you‚Äôll know how far is too far.

A popular rule of thumb is to keep CPA to one third of your LTV. And a customer should become profitable within 12 months.

**A Quick Overview  
**For each stage of your SaaS business, track these metrics:

1.  Before Product/Market Fit: Customer feedback and the product/market fit question
    
2.  Beginning to Scale: Monthly recurring revenue and churn
    
3.  Expansion: Lifetime value and cost per acquisition
    

Keep in mind that each stage is not completely exclusive. Let‚Äôs say that I‚Äôve found product/market fit and I‚Äôm starting to scale. If I‚Äôm using AdWords to acquire my customers, I‚Äôll definitely want to keep an eye on my cost per acquisition. But at this point, I‚Äôm still trying to get a handle on my churn for the first time. I don‚Äôt REALLY know how long these customers are going to stick around. So I‚Äôll check my CPA to make sure it‚Äôs somewhat reasonable (if the total revenue from a 12-month subscriber doesn‚Äôt cover it, you have a problem). Otherwise, I‚Äôll spend most of my time improving MRR and churn.

What about funnels? What about engagement metrics, ARPU, active users, number of visits to signup, and everything else? By all means, track the other metrics you need. But the above metrics are the bare minimum. Move mountains to track them before worrying about the rest. There‚Äôs little reason to track a random engagement metric if you don‚Äôt know what your MRR or churn is.
