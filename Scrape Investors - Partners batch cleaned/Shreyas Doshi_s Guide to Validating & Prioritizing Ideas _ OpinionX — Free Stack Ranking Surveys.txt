So, what really happened here?

There are many possible reasons for this saga, but the most common ones:

A. The product should not have been built in the first place

B. The original product was ill-conceived & the later pivots had to inherit this original error

Let's look at Option A üëá

The product solved _a_ problem, but not **the** problem.

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/5e22fb8d8524a16d5bd5683c/0a2dbe0c-89f4-4d8c-b9c2-6d3191ebd5e6/19.png)

Often, product teams aim to be in the top right of this quadrant ‚Üí _executing to the highest quality an idea that solves a problem for someone._

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/5e22fb8d8524a16d5bd5683c/b8d861b1-f7e9-4b5d-8a1b-1eab460eaa29/20.png)

But, this model assumes that all problems are equally important. In reality, there‚Äôs a third axis ‚Üí _the importance of the problem._

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/5e22fb8d8524a16d5bd5683c/015a156a-eb12-40e6-962d-a7815f548bd9/21.png)

And the best products tackle the problems that matter most (and do it well!)

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/5e22fb8d8524a16d5bd5683c/381e8b9e-3688-4903-af47-4acbb52998fb/22.png)

Daniel Kahneman famously described **The Focusing Illusion**, which says that ‚Äú_Nothing in life is as important as you think it is, while you are thinking about it._‚Äù

For people in business, this quote could easily be rewritten as ‚Äú_Nothing in business is as important as it actually is, while you are talking about it._‚Äù

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/5e22fb8d8524a16d5bd5683c/38034652-a066-4eaf-aebc-7464a77db33a/23.png)

When you talk to a customer about a _specific_ problem, they will naturally ‚Äúfocus‚Äù on that problem ‚Äî at the exclusion of other problems they (or their team/business) are facing.

Your focus brings a disproportionate emphasis to the discussion on solving THAT specific problem. Hence the customer‚Äôs initial excitement about your possible product solution. Hence the tepid response and adoption once you‚Äôve built said product.

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/5e22fb8d8524a16d5bd5683c/378d90e5-1428-4c33-bf41-dbf1521bb2f3/24.png)

If you were to zoom out and look at this problem in comparison to all of the other problems that this customer is currently dealing with, you might quickly realize that what _you_ thought of as a ‚Äúhair-on-fire problem‚Äù is something _they_ see as just a ‚Äúmild inconvenience‚Äù.

And **nobody goes out of their way to find solutions for a mild inconvenience.**

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/5e22fb8d8524a16d5bd5683c/b31296d2-824e-46a9-9316-684f7f96b177/25.png)

This approach is called **‚Äú**[**Customer Problems Stack Rank**](https://www.opinionx.co/blog/customer-problem-stack-ranking)**‚Äù** (CPSR). You ask the customer to stack rank the problem versus all the other problems they are trying to solve for their business and org.

This way of thinking can be applied across functions ‚Äî you should also get the CPSR from other personas involved, like the VP Support, VP Marketing, VP Sales, etc.

You are now closer to truth.

![](https://images.squarespace-cdn.com/content/v1/5e22fb8d8524a16d5bd5683c/2dc1d8ab-afb0-49bc-b3fd-47962d1f07d5/26.jpg)

When I first read Destined to Fail, I knew immediately that Shreyas was describing my EXACT circumstances. We had launched 6 months earlier and that same week had lost our only subscription customer. Things were looking pretty bleak. We had conducted about 150 interviews by that point to figure out what problem to solve, but clearly it just wasn‚Äôt working.

We decided to run one last experiment before giving up, where we would ask a bunch of our target customers to stack rank a list of problems so we could see where the key problem we were focused on solving ranked in their list of priorities.

In just one evening, we drafted a list of problem statements based on our customer discovery interviews, shared them with 600 target customers, and could already see that our problem statement was **ranking** **dead last** out of 45 options. But the biggest surprise for us was that 5 of the top 7 highest-ranking problems were actually very similar to the one we were trying to solve!

So the next morning we rewrote our landing page and product onboarding flow to match these 5 high-ranking problems instead. Within a week of starting this stack ranking experiment, we had multiple paying customers, our landing page was converting to trial 3x better than before, and we even had our first customer testimonials!
